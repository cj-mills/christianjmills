Executive Summary

John Taylor Gatto, a former New York State Teacher of the Year, argues that compulsory schooling in America is a system of “weapons of mass instruction” designed to create a docile and compliant workforce rather than educated and independent citizens.

He criticizes the emphasis on standardized testing, the artificial extension of childhood, the suppression of creativity and critical thinking, and the disconnect between schooling and real-world needs.

Gatto calls for a radical transformation of education, advocating for self-directed learning, engagement with real-world experiences, and the dismantling of the standardized testing industry through a peaceful movement of mass refusal, inspired by the spirit of Herman Melville’s “Bartleby the Scrivener.”

Prologue: Against School

Boredom in School

  • Gatto observed rampant boredom among both students and teachers.

Sudent boredom

  • Students found the work unengaging and repetitive, feeling they already knew the material.
  • They desired practical, hands-on learning experiences.
  • Students perceived teachers as uninterested in their subjects and lacking deeper knowledge.

Teacher boredom

  • Teacher boredom is commonplace, evident in the low morale and dispirited atmosphere of teacher’s lounges.
  • Teachers often blamed students’ poor behavior and fixation on grades for their own lack of enthusiasm.
  • Teachers themselves are products of the same compulsory schooling system that bored them as students.
  • The rigid structures imposed on teachers further stifle their passion and creativity.

Responsibility

  • Gatto asserts that everyone involved in the education system shares responsibility for the pervasive boredom.
  • Gatto’s grandfather instilled in him the importance of self-reliance and personal responsibility for combating boredom.
  • He believed that relying on external sources for amusement and intellectual stimulation was childish.
  • This lesson had a lasting impact on Gatto, shaping his view on education.

Challenging Educational Norms

  • Gatto challenged traditional educational practices in his classroom to help students break free from boredom.
  • Gatto’s efforts to reform education were met with resistance and hostility from the established system.
  • He faced serious consequences, including the loss of his job and teaching license, for his defiance.
  • These experiences solidified his belief that the school system prioritizes conformity over genuine learning.

Questioning the Necessity of Schooling

  • Gatto questions the necessity of the traditional schooling model, suggesting alternative approaches.
  • He advocates for flexibility in time, curriculum, and assessment methods.
  • He highlights the importance of:
    • Connecting students with knowledgeable and passionate mentors.
    • Fostering student autonomy and risk-taking.
  • Gatto poses the question: What if the current education system is intentionally designed to limit students’ potential?
  • He suggests that the system’s flaws might be intentional, serving a hidden purpose.

The success of homeschooled children and historical figures

  • He points to the success of homeschooled children and historical figures who achieved greatness without formal schooling as evidence.
  • He argues that education and forced schooling are not synonymous.

The True Purpose of Public Schools

The rise of compulsory schooling in the US and its purported goals

  • Compulsory schooling gained traction in the US between 1905 and 1915.
  • The stated goals of this system were:
    • To cultivate good character.
    • To foster responsible citizenship.
    • To help each student reach their full potential.
  • Gatto argues that these goals are misleading and mask the true purpose of compulsory education.
  • Gatto contends that the stated goals of public education are not effectively achieved.
  • He posits that alternative explanations for the existence of compulsory schooling exist.

H.L. Mencken’s critique of public education as a tool for standardization and suppression of individuality

  • Gatto cites H.L. Mencken, who argued that the true aim of public education is to create a standardized citizenry, suppress dissent, and discourage originality.
  • Mencken believed that the education system prioritizes conformity and obedience over intellectual curiosity and independent thought.

Prussian Roots of American Education

  • Mencken traced the origins of the American education system to the Prussian model, a system designed to produce obedient and intellectually limited subjects.
  • The Prussian system aimed to create a manageable populace by discouraging critical thinking and independent thought.
  • Prominent figures like William James and Orestes Brownson expressed concerns about the Prussianization of American schools.

The Influence of James Bryant Conant

  • James Bryant Conant, a highly influential figure in American education, played a key role in shaping standardized testing and promoting large, impersonal high schools.
  • Conant acknowledged a deliberate and transformative shift in American schooling between 1905 and 1930.

Alexander Inglis and the Six Functions of Schooling

  • Alexander Inglis, in his book “Principles of Secondary Education,” revealed the true intentions behind compulsory schooling.
  • Inglis outlined six functions of schooling:
    1. The Adjustive or Adaptive Function: To instill unquestioning obedience to authority.
    2. The Integrating Function: To mold students into conformity and predictability.
    3. The Diagnostic and Directive Function: To determine and assign social roles to students.
    4. The Differentiating Function: To sort and train individuals based on their predetermined social roles.
    5. The Selective Function: To weed out the “unfit” individuals through a system of grades and labels.
    6. The Propedeutic Function: To cultivate a small elite to manage the system.

The Economic Motives of Mass Schooling

  • Inglis’s ideas were widely accepted among influential figures in education and industry.
  • Mandatory schooling served to create a compliant workforce and a mass consumer base.
  • Industrialists recognized the economic benefits of a population conditioned for consumerism and obedience.

Perpetuating Childishness

  • Woodrow Wilson advocated for differentiated education based on social class, limiting opportunities for the lower classes.

    • “We want one class of persons to have a liberal education, and we want another class of persons, a very much larger class of necessity in every society, to forego the privileges of a liberal education and fit themselves to perform specific difficult manual tasks.”

  • Fear, the pursuit of efficiency, and greed were driving forces behind these decisions.

  • Mass schooling fostered a society of uncritical consumers.

  • Gatto argues that schooling intentionally infantilizes the populace, keeping them dependent and easily manipulated.

The Banishment of Maturity

  • Modern society promotes instant gratification and erodes adult responsibility.
  • People have surrendered critical thinking and independence in favor of commercial and political manipulation.

A Path to a Different Future

  • Gatto encourages parents to recognize and avoid the pitfalls of modern schooling.
  • He advocates for fostering leadership, critical thinking, and a rich inner life in children.
  • He encourages exposing children to challenging and intellectually stimulating material.
  • Gatto emphasizes the importance of raising independent thinkers and leaders, not subservient consumers.
  • He believes in empowering children to reach their full potential by rejecting the limitations imposed by the current system.

Chapter 1: Everything You Know About Schools Is Wrong

Helen Todd: Why Children Work

  • In 1909, a factory inspector’s survey of 500 working children found that 412 preferred factory work over returning to school.
    • Source: Helen Todd, “Why Children Work,” McClure’s Magazine, April 1913.
    • Archive: Why Children Work: The Children’s Answer By Helen M. Todd
  • Helen Todd received various reasons from children for favoring factory work, including:
    1. Monetary Factors:
      • Being paid for the factory work they do.
      • The ability to support their families financially, afford more food, shoes for babies, and other basic needs.
    2. Difficulty and Relevance of Schoolwork:
      • Perceived difficulty in understanding schoolwork compared to factory work.
      • Learning being perceived as hard.
      • Some children feeling unable to learn.
      • Perception that school education is not relevant to their ability to earn.
    3. The Environment at School and Factory:
      • Preference for the factory environment as there is less judgment compared to school.
      • The factory environment doesn’t involve name-calling or bullying as in school.
      • They can avoid the corporal punishment experienced at school for:
        • Failure to learn
        • Whispering
        • Having string in their pocket
        • Squeaky seats
        • Late arrival
        • Forgetting pages of the book

Running the World

Utopian Thinkers and the Shift in Family Dynamics

  • Smithsonian Archive: A Social History of the American Family From Colonial Times to the Present Volume 3
  • In 1919, Professor Arthur Calhoun’s A Social History of the American Family From Colonial Times to the Present highlighted a shift in family dynamics within schools.
    • Children were moving from biological families to the care of supposed “community experts” (page 175).
    • This change aligned with utopian thinkers’ aspirations, particularly regarding scientific population control.
    • Calhoun believed public education would help achieve the Darwinian and Galtonian ideal of selective breeding by controlling the reproduction of the “unfit.” (page 331)

Concerns about Invisible Government Influence on Schools

  • Not everyone shared Calhoun’s enthusiasm for the changes within the education system.
  • In 1922, New York City Mayor John Hyland raised concerns about an “invisible government” influencing schools.
    • He compared this influence to an octopus seizing its prey.
    • Hyland specifically identified the Rockefeller Foundation as the entity behind this covert control.
  • Hyland’s statement echoed British Prime Minister Benjamin Disraeli’s earlier warning about an invisible government controlling major events.

The Rockefeller Foundation and the Rise of Compulsory Schooling

  • The 1920s saw a significant increase in compulsory schooling, coinciding with a booming stock market.
  • The 1928 book A Sociological Philosophy of Education asserted that teachers were tasked with running not only schools, but the world.
    • Book: A Sociological Philosophy of Education by Ross Lee Finney
  • Edward Thorndike, from the Rockefeller-funded Columbia Teachers College, downplayed the importance of academic subjects in education.
    • He believed academics held “little value.”
  • William Kilpatrick, Thorndike’s colleague, advocated for experts taking over child-rearing in his book Education and the Social Crisis.
    • Kilpatrick considered family a backward institution and believed experts were better equipped to raise children.

The Control of Human Behavior

The Rockefeller Foundation’s Program for Controlling Human Behavior through Education

  • On April 11, 1933, Rockefeller Foundation President Max Mason revealed a national program aimed at controlling human behavior through education.
    • Report: The Rockefeller Foundation Annual Report (1933)
    • Book Chaper: DB Paul, The Rockefeller Foundation and the Origins of Behavioral Genetics. In K Benson, et al., eds., The Expansion of American Biology (New Brunswick, NJ: Rutgers Univ. Press, 1991), 263-283.

Max Muller’s Influence on Eugenics and Population Control

  • Eastern European geneticist Max Muller significantly influenced the Rockefeller Foundation’s investment in controlling human evolution.
    • Muller’s research involved using X-rays to manipulate fruit fly genetics, aiming to control mutations.
  • Muller, like Darwin and Galton before him, believed that controlling human breeding was key to achieving a utopian society.
    • This belief was shared by prominent scientists and powerful economic entities.
  • Muller, who later won the Nobel Prize, summarized his ideas in the “Geneticists’ Manifesto.”
    • The manifesto, signed by 22 American and British biologists, proposed separating “worthwhile breeding stock” from the general population.

The Geneticists’ Manifesto and the Eugenics Movement

The Geneticists’ Manifesto and its Implications for Social Engineering

  • The “Geneticists’ Manifesto” brought previously private discussions about eugenics into public discourse.
  • This marked a departure from democratic and egalitarian ideals.
    • F. Scott Fitzgerald alluded to this shift in his novel The Great Gatsby.

The End of Horace Mann’s Common School Ideal

  • The concept of separating students based on intelligence contradicted Horace Mann’s common school ideal, which aimed for social harmony through mixed-ability classrooms.
    • Mann’s idea was effectively abandoned, except in rhetoric.
  • An executive director of the National Education Association stated that the NEA aimed to achieve through education what European dictators sought through force.

World War II and the Post-War Education Battleground

  • World War II forced the eugenics movement underground.
  • Following the war, a battle emerged between traditional education and a new pedagogical order driven by corporate non-profit foundations like Carnegie and Rockefeller.
  • Two congressional investigations, in 1915 and 1959, concluded that school policy was being shaped by corporate interests, away from public scrutiny.
    • These reports received minimal public attention and were largely ignored by teacher’s colleges.

The Transformation of Teacher Training

  • Between 1967 and 1974, teacher training underwent a radical transformation driven by private foundations, universities, think tanks, government agencies, and corporations.
    • This change was coordinated through the U.S. Office of Education and key state education departments.

Three Milestones: Designing Education for the Future, B.S.T.E.P., and Bloom’s Taxonomy

  • Three significant developments in teacher training transformation:
    1. Designing Education for the Future: This government initiative redefined education as a means to achieve national economic and social goals, omitting any mention of personal goals.
      • Education Resources Information Center: Designing Education for the Future: An Eight-State Project
    2. The Behavioral Science Teacher Education Project (B.S.T.E.P.): This project outlined reforms to be implemented after 1967, including manipulating individuals through education and normalizing chemical experimentation on minors.
      • Feasibility Study: Behavioral Science Teacher Education Program. Final Report
    3. Bloom’s Taxonomy of Educational Objectives: This multi-volume manual by Benjamin Bloom provided a framework for classifying student behavior and shaping their thoughts, feelings, and actions.
      • Book: Taxonomy of Educational Objectives, Handbook 1: Cognitive Domain

Behavioral Psychology and the Classroom as a Laboratory

  • Schools became laboratories for behavioral psychology experiments on children, often without public knowledge or consent.

  • These experiments involved testing children to assess their mental susceptibility.

  • Bloom’s Taxonomy provided a tool for classifying student behavior and controlling their minds and actions.

  • Bloom-inspired programs aimed to generate data for social engineering purposes.

  • B.S.T.E.P. envisioned a future with:

    • Multipurpose identification numbers for tracking individuals.
    • Controlled opinions through direct and subliminal influence.
    • An elite controlling society, with limited participatory democracy.
    • A focus on attitudes and skills compatible with a non-work world.

  • These projects transformed classrooms into laboratories of psychological manipulation.

Teachers as Therapists

  • A surge in school violence in the mid-to-late 1960s, partly attributed to a Ford Foundation policy requiring due process in disciplining students, contributed to the transformation of schools into laboratories.
    • Due process procedures, being slow and deliberate, proved ineffective in addressing immediate student misbehavior.
  • B.S.T.E.P. called for training teachers as therapists, applying social psychology principles in the classroom.
  • This marked a shift in the curriculum’s focus.

We Don’t Need Brains

Industrialists and Financiers Shaping Education Through Funding

  • Between 1896 and 1920, industrialists and financiers significantly influenced the development of forced schooling through funding.
    • Andrew Carnegie and John D. Rockefeller were particularly influential.

The Rockefeller General Education Board’s Vision for Education

  • A statement from Frederick T. Gates of Rockefeller’s General Education Board revealed their vision for education 1906 “Occasional Letter No. 1”:

    • “In our dream, we have limitless resources, and the people yield themselves with perfect docility to our molding hand. The present educational conventions fade from our minds; and, unhampered by tradition, we work our own good will upon a grateful and responsive rural folk. We shall not try to make these people or any of their children into philosophers or men of learning or of science. We are not to raise up from among them authors, orators, poets, or men of letters. We shall not search for embryo great artists, painters, musicians. Nor will we cherish even the humbler ambition to raise up from among them lawyers, doctors, preachers, politicians, statesmen, of whom we now have ample supply.”

    • Book: The country school of to-morrow

  • The General Education Board’s statement raises questions about the identity of the “we” seeking to control education.

Is This Nuts?

World War II Literacy Rates and the Subsequent Decline

  • At the start of World War II, literacy rates among inductees were high, with 96% demonstrating sufficient reading skills.
  • Literacy rates saw a significant decline in subsequent years.
    • 19% illiteracy in the Korean War draft pool.
    • 27% illiteracy in the Vietnam War draft pool.
  • This decline in literacy occurred despite increased years of schooling and more professionally trained teachers.
  • The decline in literacy during the Vietnam War was particularly alarming, with a significant portion of inductees struggling to read even basic materials.
  • SAT scores are unreliable indicators of literacy due to frequent re-norming or inflation.
  • By 2000, the illiteracy rate for Black Americans was reported at 40%, while the rate for white Americans was 17%, despite a 350% increase in education spending.

    • This argument ignores contradictory data, such as the lower rates of violence among Black South Africans compared to Black Americans, despite similar population sizes.
    • Additionally, Jamaica reported a 98.5% literacy rate in 2004, significantly higher than the 83% rate for white Americans in 2005.

The Shift to Non-Phonetic Reading Instruction

  • One explanation for declining literacy rates is the shift from phonetic to non-phonetic reading instruction in American public schools.
    • White Americans, with a longer history of literacy, had alternative learning resources at home, unlike many Black Americans who were historically denied access to education.
  • When schools abandoned phonetic reading instruction, Black students lacked the same support systems that benefited white students.
  • The U.S. Army recognized this issue during World War II, as many recruits failed literacy tests.
    • Despite this, the education system did not revert to phonetic instruction.

Dumbing Down the Curriculum

  • In 1995, a Minneapolis teacher highlighted the significant decline in curriculum standards by comparing a 19th-century fifth-grade reader containing works by Shakespeare, Thoreau, and other renowned authors to a contemporary curriculum where students struggled to spell basic words.

William Torrey Harris and His Influence on American Education

  • William Torrey Harris, U.S. Commissioner of Education from 1889 to 1906, played a pivotal role in shaping American education.

    • Harris, a Hegelian philosopher and friend of Andrew Carnegie, advocated for state control over children’s education and promoted a system that prioritized obedience and conformity.

  • Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel: A Prussian philosopher who molded Karl Marx and J. P. Morgan

    • Taught that history could be deliberately manipulated by provoking crises out of public view and then demanding national unity to meet those crises.

  • Harris aimed to create a society devoid of independent thought and action, where individuals passively awaited instructions.

  • His strategy involved alienating children from themselves, their families, traditions, and culture, leaving the state as the sole authority.

  • Harris believed that schools should instill obedience and conformity, stripping individuals of their individuality.

    • He stated, “Ninety-nine students out of a hundred are automata, careful to walk in prescribed paths, careful to follow the prescribed custom. This is not an accident but the result of substantial education which, scientifically defined, is the subsumption of the individual.”
    • Source: The Philosophy of Education, 1906.

The Logic of Self-Alienation for an Industrial Economy

  • Self-alienation served the interests of a mass-production, industrial-commercial economy.

    • A workforce conditioned to accept their lot and seek fulfillment outside of work posed less of a threat to the established order.

  • A shallow inner life made it easier for individuals to cope with the tedium of unfulfilling work, reducing the likelihood of dissent.

  • Harris’ vision for education, as implemented, undermined the American experiment by prioritizing obedience and conformity over individual liberty and critical thinking.

The Crisis of Democracy

  • By the late 19th century, influential figures sought to use education to shape young minds and instill specific ideologies that served their interests.
  • Wealthy individuals and families, recognizing the potential of education to either maintain or disrupt the status quo, heavily influenced the development of the education system.

Eliminating Local Voices

  • The U.S. Bureau of Education, in its 1872 “Circular of Information,” expressed concern that educating workers would make them more aware of their grievances and more likely to challenge authority.

  • This concern was echoed in an 1885 Senate Committee report, which stated, “We believe that education is one of the principal causes of discontent of late years manifesting itself among the laboring classes.”

  • To control the potential for dissent, efforts were made to centralize control over education and limit local influence.

    • Local school boards, initially numbering around 135,000, were gradually consolidated, reducing their number to 15,000 within a century.
    • This consolidation eroded community oversight and made it easier for special interest groups to influence education policy.

  • The consolidation of school districts further eroded local control and made it easier for centralized authorities to dictate policy.

  • This consolidation was often justified as a cost-saving measure, but in reality, it led to increased costs and reduced accountability.

A Different Agenda

  • Andrew Carnegie, in his book The Empire of Business, argued against educating the working class, claiming it fostered discontent and instilled impractical ideas.
  • Max Otto, in his 1949 essay “Science and the Moral Life,” acknowledged the influence of business interests on education.
    • He attributed this influence to the rise of mass production and the need to create demand for goods and services.
  • The new system of forced schooling prioritized obedience and conformity over independent thought and self-reliance.
    • Students were trained to consume knowledge passively rather than generate their own ideas.
  • Education shifted from fostering independent livelihoods to preparing students for employment, with an emphasis on securing “good jobs.”

The Loss of America’s Libertarian Educational Heritage

  • This shift led to a decline in alternative education models that had previously flourished.

  • Figures like Benjamin Franklin, who thrived in a system of open-source learning, became less common.

  • Brownson believed that influential figures were intentionally reshaping American society to align with British and German models.

The Fourth Purpose

  • Education has evolved to serve corporate interests, even though government-mandated schooling was not explicitly mentioned in the Constitution.

    • Jobs project: It creates a vast number of jobs, making the education system a major employer.

Overproduction and the Need for Controlled Education

  • Gatto links the rise of compulsory schooling to the concept of “overproduction,” a concern for industrialists in the late 19th and early 20th centuries.
  • He argues that an educated, independent, and innovative populace posed a threat to established economic interests by:
    • Increasing competition: Independent producers and entrepreneurs challenged large corporations.
    • Driving down prices: An abundance of goods and services could lead to price wars and reduced profits.
    • Disrupting established industries: New inventions and ideas could render existing businesses obsolete.
  • To combat this, he suggests, schools were redesigned to:
    • Stifle innovation: Discouraging independent thought and creativity.
    • Promote consumption: Shifting focus from production to consuming goods and services.
    • Create a compliant workforce: Producing workers conditioned for obedience and less likely to challenge the status quo.

    1. Tolerance of disruptive behavior: Attributing it to bad teaching and undermining discipline.
    2. Recruitment of disruptive students: Concealing declining enrollment by introducing chaos into classrooms.
  • Gatto observes that administrators prioritize self-preservation and maintaining the status quo over genuine improvement.

School as a Narcotic, Creating Addicted Spectators

  • Gatto argues that the education system, through its emphasis on passive learning, creates an addiction to entertainment and a detachment from reality.
  • Children are described as “nightmare children,” lacking vital interests and preferring fantasy to exploration and real-world engagement.
  • Gatto posits that schools, rather than fostering active learning, have become agents of passivity, hindering personal growth and meaningful engagement with the world.

Conclusion and Call to Action

  • Gatto concludes by expressing his intention to circulate the letter to the new school board, hoping to provoke reflection and change.
  • Despite skepticism about the likelihood of meaningful action, Gatto feels compelled to speak out against the failings of the education system.

Chapter 5: Hector Isn’t the Problem

I Quit, I Think

  • Gatto wrote a short essay titled “I Quit, I Think” and submitted it to the Wall Street Journal.

  • Gatto’s essay outlined his reasons for leaving teaching, even without financial security.

  • The essay argued:

    “Government schooling is the most radical adventure in history. It kills the family by monopolizing the best times of childhood and by teaching disrespect for home and parents.”

    • The structure of schooling is compared to an “Egyptian” pyramid, representing the idea that “human value is a scarce thing.”

    • This concept, passed down through the Puritans and reflected in the bell curve, is presented as a “religious notion” with school as its “church.”

    • Gatto argues that he, as a teacher, enforces rituals and provides documentation to maintain this system.

    • He cites Socrates’ prediction that formalizing teaching would lead to the profession protecting its interests by making learning seem more complicated than it is.

    • School is described as a “jobs project,” “contract giver,” and “protector of the social order” resistant to meaningful reform.

    • Gatto criticizes the arbitrary nature of school timelines, using the example of two children learning to read at different ages:

      “David learns to read at age four. Rachel at age nine. In normal development, when both are thirteen, you can’t tell which one learned first. The five-year spread means nothing at all. But in school, I label Rachel learning disabled, and slow David down a bit, too.”

    • He argues that labeling children as “learning disabled” or “gifted and talented” are “sacred myths” that perpetuate the school system rather than reflecting true learning differences.

    • Gatto criticizes standardized testing, bells, uniform time blocks, age grading, and standardization as punishments inflicted by the “school religion.”

    • He advocates for individualized learning:

      “There isn’t a right way to become educated. There are as many ways as there are fingerprints. We don’t need state-certified teachers to make education happen. Certification probably guarantees it won’t.”

    • Gatto calls for a “free market” approach to education, with diverse options and risk-taking.

    • He criticizes national curricula and testing, viewing them as arising from “ignorance of how people learn or deliberate indifference to it.”

    • The essay concludes with:

      “I can’t teach this way any longer. If you hear of a job where I don’t have to hurt kids to make a living, let me know. Come fall, I’ll be looking for work.”

  • Gatto submitted the essay in March and “forgot it.”

  • He received a note indicating it might be published at the editor’s discretion but soon forgot about it.

  • On July 5, 1991, Gatto officially resigned from teaching.

  • Twenty days later, the Wall Street Journal published his essay.

  • Reflecting on his career, Gatto expresses disbelief at spending so much time in an “institutionalized” system:

    “Looking back on a thirty-year teaching career full of rewards and prizes, somehow I can’t completely believe that I spent so much of my time on earth institutionalized. I can’t believe that centralized schooling is allowed to exist at all as a giant indoctrinating and sorting machine robbing people of their children. Did it really happen? Was this my life? God help me. School is a religion.”

School as a Religion and Its Impact on Society

  • Gatto argues that to understand the education system, one must recognize its “holy mission” aspect.

  • He believes that factors like human error, greed, or class conflict are less important than the system’s fundamental nature.

  • Gatto contends:

    “Ordinary people send their children to school to get smart, but what modern schooling teaches is dumbness.”

  • He differentiates between old-fashioned “ignorance” and modern “dumbness”:

    • Modern dumbness is categorized into “permanent mathematical categories of relative stupidity,” such as “gifted and talented, mainstream, and special ed.”
    • This categorization, according to Gatto, rations learning to maintain the system and social order.

  • Gatto argues:

    “Dumb people are no longer merely ignorant. Now they are dangerous imbeciles whose minds must be conditioned with substantial doses of commercially prepared disinformation for tranquilizing purposes.”

  • He suggests that this “new dumbness” is particularly harmful to middle and upper-middle-class children, who are already pressured to conform.

  • These children, despite their degrees and licenses, often lack a strong sense of self and purpose, leading to potential issues later in life.

  • Gatto cites English historian Alan Bullock:

    “Alan Bullock, the English historian, said evil is a state of incompetence. If he’s right, then our school adventure filled the twentieth century with evil.”

  • He argues that schooling creates dependence on external validation, citing a family member’s aspiration to be “a small part in a great machine” as heartbreaking.

  • Gatto believes that “dumbing down” children through schooling leaves them unable to think independently and constantly seeking approval.

  • He challenges various explanations for the existence of “dumb” people:

    • Biological: The bell curve model.
    • Socioeconomic: The neo-Marxist model.
    • Moral: The Calvinist model.
    • Evolutionary: The Darwinian model.
    • Social Hierarchy: The pragmatic elitist model.
    • Spiritual: The Buddhist model.

  • Gatto posits that the very concept of widespread “dumbness” is a fabrication:

    “The possibility that dumb people don’t exist in sufficient numbers to warrant the many careers devoted to tending them may seem incredible to you, yet that is my proposition. Mass dumbness first had to be imagined. It isn’t real.”

Hector Rodriguez

  • Gatto introduces Hector Rodriguez, a 13-year-old student:

    • “Slightly built, olive-skinned, short, with huge black eyes.”

  • He describes an incident where he witnessed Hector attempting to slip through the gate of a skating rink despite having a paid ticket.

  • Hector’s explanation reveals he was conducting an experiment to see if he could bypass the turnstile.

  • Gatto’s research into Hector’s school records reveals a history of minor infractions labeled as “outlaw” behavior.

  • Hector attended one of the lowest-rated public schools in New York State.

  • The school rigidly categorized students into five categories:

    • Gifted and Talented Honors
    • Gifted and Talented
    • Special Progress
    • Mainstream
    • Special Ed

  • Gatto highlights the financial incentive for schools to label students as “Special Ed,” as they received three times more funding for those students.

  • Hector was placed in “Mainstream D,” the lowest rung above “Special Ed.”

  • His standardized test scores placed him “three years behind the middle of the pack.”

  • After the skating rink incident, Hector was arrested at his former elementary school with a fake gun.

  • His intention was to “turn the younger children loose,” to “free the slaves” like Spartacus.

  • A year later, Hector’s high school record showed failure in every subject and truancy.

  • Gatto challenges the reader to see Hector based on his school records:

    • “Poor, small for his age, a member of a minority. Not paid much attention by people who matter. Dumb in a super-dumb class, a bizarre gate-crasher, a gunslinger, a total failure in high school. Can you see Hector?”

  • He argues that the system encourages this limited and predictable view of Hector.

  • Gatto presents Hector as representative of countless children deemed problematic by the education system:

    “This is the boy, multiplied by millions, whom school people agonized about in every decade of the 20th century. This is the boy who destroyed the academic mission of American public schooling, turning it into a warehouse operation, a clinic for behavioral training and attitude adjustment.”

  • He recounts a statement made by his school principal during a documentary filming:

    “Sure, the system stinks, but John has nothing to replace it. And bad as the system is, it’s better than chaos.”

Challenging the Notion of Hector as the Problem

  • Gatto questions whether “chaos” is the only alternative to the existing education system.

  • He argues:

    “The country has been sold the idea Hector is the problem of modern schooling.”

  • Gatto asserts that this misconception is the true issue:

    “That misperception is the demon we face under its many guises and behind its shape-shifting rhetoric.”

  • He believes that forced schooling was designed to control children like Hector.

  • Gatto concludes:

    “Important people believe, with the fervor of religious zealots, that civilization can only survive if the irrational, unpredictable impulses of human nature are continually beaten back and confined until their demonic vitality is sapped.”

A Guerrilla Curriculum for Real-World Engagement

Chapter 7: Weapons of Mass Instruction

A Moral Odor

Jacques Lusserand’s critique of schooling

  • Jacques Lusserand, a blind French teenager who led a resistance group during World War II, critiqued his schooling in his autobiography, And Then There Was Light.

“There is such a thing as a moral odor, and that was the case at school. A group of human beings that stay in one room by compulsion begins to smell. That is literally the case, and with children it happens even faster. Just think how much suppressed anger, humiliated independence, frustrated vagrancy, and impotent curiosity could be accumulated by boys between the ages of 10 and 14.” - Jacques Lusserand, And Then There Was Light

  • Lusserand found the classroom experience to be a “moral disaster,” comparing it to a room with a foul odor.
  • He believed that forced confinement of children in school led to negative emotions and stifled natural instincts.

The impact of school on moral development

  • Lusserand’s ability to commit violent acts shortly after leaving school, where “the world of reality with all its real moral questions was entirely lacking,” raises questions about the impact of schooling on moral development.
  • Gatto suggests a connection between the school environment and events like the Columbine High School massacre, using the phrase, “We become what we behold.”

School as a Weapon

Historical negativity towards schooling

  • Gatto notes that historical accounts of schooling are overwhelmingly negative, prompting the question of why such a system persists.
  • While acknowledging potential benefits, Gatto questions the trade-offs of schooling, particularly its impact on relationships.

School’s emphasis on winning over learning

  • Gatto argues that schools prioritize winning over learning to the point where students often graduate with limited knowledge, despite the institution’s stated goals.

Societal implications of compulsory schooling

  • Gatto contends that the practice of compulsory schooling, where children are entrusted to strangers for instruction in an unclear curriculum, reveals a significant flaw in modern culture.

  • Gatto highlights negative portrayals of schooling in various cultural artifacts:

    • Horace’s ode: Describes the torments of schooling.
    • Pompeii mosaics: Illustrate harsh school discipline.
    • “The Legend of Sleepy Hollow”: Celebrates a protagonist who gets revenge on an unbearable schoolmaster.
    • “School Days”: A song fondly depicting a connection between learning and corporal punishment.

    • Websites: Numerous online platforms dedicated to disrupting school routines.

  • Gatto contrasts this with the rarity of individuals attributing their success to their time in school.

A Personal Formula

School as a source of damage

  • Gatto, drawing from 30 years of teaching experience, asserts that school inflicts damage upon students, although the specific mechanisms are not fully understood.

Examining the punitive machinery of school

  • Gatto aims to identify specific aspects of the “punitive machinery” within the school system.

Gatto’s realization of school’s detrimental effects

  • Gatto experienced a realization early in his teaching career that intellectual and creative potential, as well as good character, were being diminished in the classroom.
  • He felt that he was hired to enforce this system, comparing himself to a prison guard upholding rules and procedures that stifled students.

Sabotaging the school system

  • Inspired by Jacques Lusserand, Gatto decided to “sabotage” the school system through various means.

Developing personalized curricula for students

  • Gatto developed a method for creating personalized curricula for his students, involving the following steps:
    1. Assembling a detailed biography: Gathering information about each student’s life, relationships, experiences, and aspirations from various sources, including family and friends.
    2. Incorporating student wishes and weaknesses: Asking each student to list three things they wanted to learn and three weaknesses they wanted to overcome by the end of the year.
  • Gatto implemented this personalized approach without consulting school administrators, relying on the support of parents.
  • This approach resulted in increased student motivation and engagement, as each student had a personal stake in their education.

School’s belief in social order and determinism

  • Gatto argues that schools operate on a belief in a fixed social order and determinism, rejecting the idea that feedback and adaptation can lead to growth.

Incorporating student wishes and weaknesses

  • Gatto emphasizes the importance of incorporating student’s individual desires and areas for improvement into their education.

Gatto’s research on the history of schooling

  • Driven by his observations, Gatto embarked on a research project to understand the origins, evolution, and widespread adoption of the school system.

  • He concluded that the school system resembled a “utopian science fiction” experiment that disregards human needs.

  • Gatto’s research culminated in a book titled The Underground History of American Education, which a major publisher later refused to release, fearing it would damage relationships with those in the education industry.

  • In addition to his research, Gatto engaged in extensive “field exercises” with his students in New York City.

  • These exercises aimed to foster independence, self-reliance, critical thinking, communication skills, courage, and curiosity.

  • The goal was to help students overcome the limitations imposed by traditional schooling and encourage them to take ownership of their education.

Bad intentions

Examining the public assumptions of schooling

  • While not explicitly stating that schools operate with malicious intent, Gatto challenges the assumption that poor educational outcomes are inevitable, arguing that they are a direct result of the system’s design and regulations.

  • Gatto posits that schools, like other systems, resist change and maintain their integrity by suppressing dissent and discouraging feedback.

  • Gatto compares schools to other institutions that prioritize self-preservation over their stated missions.

  • Gatto cites historical examples of institutions that resisted innovation and punished those who challenged the established order, such as medieval craft guilds.

    1. Cowardice

  • Gatto argues that collectivization in education leads to a focus on the lowest common denominator, hindering the potential of gifted students.

The failure of top-down reform efforts

  • Gatto contends that top-down reform efforts in education often fail because they do not address the fundamental flaws of the system.

The importance of connectedness for education

  • Gatto emphasizes the importance of connectedness for true education, arguing that students thrive when they can connect with:
    • Different human styles and perspectives
    • Complex, real-world experiences
    • A wide range of intellectual ideas
    • Themselves through self-knowledge

THE TALKING CHOO-CHOO SYNDROME

Gatto’s experience with a private school curriculum

  • Gatto recounts his experience reviewing the curriculum of a private school, noting both positive aspects and areas of concern.

The separation of students, staff, and administration

  • Gatto observed a disconnect between students, teachers, and administrators, hindering open communication and collaboration.

The bowdlerization of classic literature

  • Gatto criticizes the practice of simplifying and “sanitizing” classic literature for students, arguing that it deprives them of the richness and complexity of the original works.

Gatto’s critique of a talking choo-choo workbook

  • Gatto expresses his concern about a workbook that relies on a “talking choo-choo” as a pedagogical tool, viewing it as a symptom of a larger trend toward infantilizing education.

The artificial extension of childhood and its consequences

  • Gatto argues that modern society artificially extends childhood, hindering the development of independence and critical thinking.

The ineffectiveness of school-based anti-smoking programs

The manageability of helpless individuals

  • Gatto suggests that extending childhood and fostering dependence makes individuals more manageable and easier to control.

The pervasiveness of talking choo-choos in modern culture

  • Gatto uses the “talking choo-choo” as a metaphor for the infantilization of culture, citing examples like mindless entertainment and consumerism.

The historical roots of the talking choo-choo syndrome

  • Gatto traces the roots of the “talking choo-choo” syndrome to the writings of John Calvin, Baruch Spinoza, and Johann Gottlieb Fichte, as well as the rise of the welfare state.

Beatrix Potter’s rejection of goody-goody books and her embrace of realism

  • Gatto highlights the work of children’s author Beatrix Potter, known for her tales of Peter Rabbit and other animals.
  • He praises Potter for her realism and willingness to address difficult themes in her stories, contrasting her approach with the trend of oversimplifying and sanitizing children’s literature.

Detach the Training Wheels

The case of Alina Del Don and her early independence

  • Gatto shares the story of Alina Del Don, a talented basketball player who, at the age of three, independently removed the training wheels from her bike.
  • He uses this anecdote to illustrate the innate capabilities of children and the importance of fostering independence.

The importance of recognizing individual potential

Gatto’s advice to parents: treat children as human beings

  • Gatto offers advice to parents:
    • Treat children as capable human beings.
    • Abandon the notion of extended childhood.
    • Engage children in open and honest conversations about family finances and other real-world matters.
    • Encourage independence and self-reliance.

The fabricated nature of adolescence

  • Gatto challenges the concept of adolescence as a distinct developmental stage, arguing that it is a socially constructed phenomenon.

The case of John Latsis and his success without formal education

  • Gatto shares the story of John Latsis, a self-made billionaire who achieved success without formal education, starting as a laborer at age 12 and eventually building a shipping empire.

Tanya Eby and George Meehan

Tanya Eby’s solo circumnavigation and her rejection of schooling

  • Gatto tells the story of Tanya Eby, who, at age 17, circumnavigated the globe solo, becoming the first woman known to do so.
  • He highlights that Eby dropped out of high school and undertook this challenge despite facing difficulties in the traditional education system.

George Meehan’s longest walk and his lack of formal education

  • Gatto shares the story of George Meehan, who completed the longest walk in recorded history, traveling from the southern tip of South America to Point Barrow, Alaska.
  • He emphasizes that Meehan achieved this feat with minimal schooling and resources, demonstrating the power of determination and resilience.

The lessons learned from dropouts and self-directed individuals

  • Gatto uses the stories of Tanya Eby and George Meehan to illustrate the limitations of traditional education and the potential of self-directed learning.

The contrast between schooled professionals and adventurous dropouts

  • Gatto contrasts the lives of Tanya Eby and George Meehan with those of highly educated professionals who engage in escapist behaviors, suggesting that true fulfillment comes from pursuing one’s passions and challenging oneself.

The Trapped Flea Principle

The passivity of school children and the suppression of their spirit

  • Gatto questions why schoolchildren often exhibit passivity and indifference towards their education and future prospects.

Andrew Hsu’s explanation of flea training

  • Gatto shares an anecdote about Andrew Hsu, an 11-year-old homeschooler who won a science fair for his genetics research.
  • Hsu attributed his success to a lesson his father taught him about flea training: Fleas kept in a container with a lid will eventually learn to jump only low enough to avoid hitting the lid, even after the lid is removed.
  • This analogy highlights how limitations, whether real or perceived, can condition individuals to accept a restricted view of their potential.

The self-imposed limitations of a conditioned mind

School as a mechanism for breaking students’ spirits

  • Gatto suggests that schools, through their emphasis on conformity and limitations, condition students to accept a diminished sense of their capabilities.

How to Drive a Horse Slightly Insane

The stunting effects of schooling on human potential

  • Gatto argues that the time spent in schooling hinders the development of human potential, leaving individuals passive and unable to fully realize their capabilities.

The strategy of using negativity to control individuals

  • Gatto draws a parallel between horse training techniques and schooling practices, suggesting that both can be used to control and manipulate individuals.

The parallels between horse training and schooling

  • Gatto highlights the similarities between methods for inducing “learned helplessness” in horses and the practices employed in traditional schooling:
    • Isolation: Keeping horses isolated from one another mirrors the separation and competition fostered among students.
    • Restriction of natural behaviors: Denying horses the opportunity to engage in natural behaviors, such as grazing and socializing, echoes the limitations placed on students’ freedom of movement and interaction.

School’s role in creating consumers rather than contributors

  • Gatto argues that schools primarily function to create compliant consumers rather than independent thinkers and producers.

Boredom and consumption as tools for social control

  • Gatto suggests that boredom, coupled with a diminished imagination and a focus on external rewards, drives consumerism and makes individuals easier to control.

The Cauldron of Broken Time

The negative effects of constant interruptions on learning

  • Gatto argues that the constant interruptions and fragmented nature of the school day hinder deep learning and critical thinking.

School’s chaotic environment and its impact on concentration

  • Gatto describes the chaotic and disruptive environment of a typical school, arguing that it makes sustained focus and concentration nearly impossible.

The importance of uninterrupted time for thought and reflection

  • Gatto emphasizes the importance of uninterrupted time for:
    • Synthesizing information
    • Forming connections between ideas
    • Engaging in deep thought and reflection

The End

A call to action: continue weapon-hunting on your own

  • Gatto concludes by encouraging readers to continue questioning the education system and identifying the “weapons of mass instruction” that hinder true learning and human potential.

Chapter 8: What is Education?

Kant’s Questions in the Epic of Europe

Kant’s Questions and the Irony of Modern Education

  • German philosopher Immanuel Kant posed four fundamental questions about education:
    • What can I know?
    • What may I hope?
    • What ought I to do?
    • What is man?
  • Ironically, the German education system, widely adopted globally, stifled philosophical curiosity.
  • American compulsory schooling is not homegrown but based on the German model, as evidenced by Horace Mann’s seventh letter to the Boston School Committee.
  • Addressing Kant’s questions is crucial for developing a meaningful curriculum.
  • Neglecting this responsibility leads to education based on others’ priorities, hindering personal growth.
  • True education transcends the pursuit of money and fame, fostering contentment through self-discovery.
  • Reflecting on diverse perspectives on education can illuminate one’s own truth.

Defining Education: Three Probes into the Mystery

  • Defining education is challenging, but exploring different perspectives can be insightful.
  • This chapter explores three perspectives:
    • Travel writer James Salter’s observations on Europe
    • John Taylor Gatto’s testimony to the U.S. Senate
    • Gatto’s free verse dedicated to his granddaughter Christina

James Salter on Europe: An Unfathomable Classroom

  • James Salter found that Europe provided a unique education beyond formal schooling.
  • “The thing it finally gave me was education, not the lessons of school, but something more elevated, a view of how to endure, how to have leisure, food, love, and conversation, how to look at nakedness, architecture, streets, all new and seeking to be thought of in a different way.” - James Salter
  • Europe’s rich history fosters humility and a sense of perspective.
  • “In Europe, the shadow of history falls upon you, and knowing none of it, you realize suddenly how small you are. To know nothing is to have done nothing. To remember only yourself is like worshipping a dust moat.” - James Salter
  • Europe offers an immersive and boundless educational experience.
  • Salter challenges readers to reflect on their own education and whether it provided a deep understanding of their surroundings.

Testimony to the U.S. Senate: A Vision for Education in 2000

  • John Taylor Gatto presented his vision for education in the year 2000 to the U.S. Senate Committee on Labor and Human Relations on October 23, 1991.
  • He argued that schools in 2000 would likely resemble those of 1990 and 1890, highlighting the stagnation in the education system.
  • Gatto contended that true education was possible in 1790 because schooling was voluntary, short, and did not dominate children’s time or family life.
  • He believed meaningful education reform was unlikely due to political systems influenced by financial interests invested in maintaining the status quo.
  • Gatto expressed pessimism about top-down reform, suggesting that change would likely originate from individuals, particularly homeschooling families.
  • He criticized the emphasis on standardized test scores, arguing they do not correlate with valuable skills or success in life.
  • “Every president of the United States, since such testing was launched, has had a mediocre to poor standardized test score. The same is more true than not among corporate executives.” - John Taylor Gatto
  • Gatto called for a national debate to define the characteristics of an educated person, emphasizing the need for clear goals.
  • He outlined his vision of an educated person:
    • Time Management: Comfortable with solitude and utilizing time effectively.
    • Relationships: Capable of forming healthy attachments and understanding relationship dynamics.
    • Mortality: Accepting and understanding the significance of their own mortality and lifelong learning.
    • Values: Possessing a strong personal value system, critically evaluating external influences, and respecting cultural diversity.
    • Creativity: Able to generate new ideas, things, and experiences.
    • Truth-Seeking: Discovering truth through evidence-based reasoning, not memorization.
    • Contribution: Utilizing their skills to meet societal needs and earn a living, while maintaining a healthy perspective on material wealth.
    • Variety and Home: Seeking enriching experiences while recognizing the importance of home and responsibilities.
  • Gatto proposed a curriculum based on fundamental life experiences:
    • Self-Discovery: Understanding one’s origins, family, culture, and personal limits and possibilities.
    • Physical World: Experiential exploration and analysis of the environment.
    • Relationships: Navigating various forms of human connection, including family, friendships, and community.
    • Vocation: Finding a fulfilling and socially valuable career path.
    • Maturity: Understanding the transition from childhood to adulthood and the accompanying responsibilities.
    • Death: Contemplating death as a natural part of life and its implications for living a meaningful life.
  • Gatto criticized the reliance on confinement schooling, highlighting the limitations of learning within a controlled environment.

A Call for a New School: Dismantling the Walled Compound

  • Gatto advocated for a decentralized education model that breaks free from traditional school structures, comparing them to “walled compounds.”
  • He argued that this new model should:
    • Eliminate reliance on certified teachers, allowing anyone with valuable skills to share their knowledge.
    • Embrace technology, particularly the internet, to facilitate learning.
    • Empower taxpayers by giving them control over educational spending.
    • Abolish standardized testing and its harmful effects on student ranking and learning.
    • Promote flexible learning environments, schedules, and personalized pacing.
    • Foster imagination and creativity by moving away from standardization.
    • Reduce reliance on school time, personnel, and materials.
    • Integrate learning into community life.
    • Encourage “productive sabotage” by educators and families to dismantle the existing system from within.
  • Gatto emphasized that learning essential skills like reading, writing, and arithmetic can occur naturally and effectively outside of formal instruction.
  • He drew parallels between learning to drive and other complex skills, arguing that competence-based assessment is more effective than traditional grading.
  • Gatto concluded by urging senators to challenge the status quo and prioritize the needs of students over entrenched financial interests.

A Free Verse for Christina: The Essence of True Education

  • Gatto’s free verse for his granddaughter, Christina, encapsulates his vision for education:
    • Individuality: Fostering uniqueness and independence.
    • Courage: Equipping individuals to face challenges and uphold principles.
    • Purpose: Guiding individuals to discover their passions and meaning in life.
    • Resilience: Cultivating the strength to navigate difficulties.
    • Life and Death: Understanding the significance of both life and death.

Chapter 9: A Letter to My Granddaughter About Dartmouth

Family Matters

  • Gatto’s granddaughter, Christina, is a rebellious and independent spirit.
    • She rode her bike on 5th Avenue despite a city ban.
    • She was captain of a national champion debating team.
    • She legally changed her name from Goodron to Christina.
  • Gatto reflects on his family’s history of being “boat rockers” and non-conformists.

Janet’s Outlawed Clan

  • Gatto’s wife, Janet, comes from a family of rebels.
    • Her ancestors were outlawed by the British Crown.
    • One of her ancestors was a woman who wore a top hat.
    • Her clan has a connection to the famous outlaw Rob Roy.
  • Gatto and Janet got married in a Buddhist temple in New York City.
    • They chose this location because they were broke and the Buddhists offered a free ceremony.
    • The temple housed a bronze Buddha statue that survived the atomic bombing of Hiroshima.
  • After they married, Janet was fired from her job for trying to organize a union.
  • Gatto was fired from his job on Madison Avenue for his inability to use a three-hole punch properly.

The Gatto Family Legacy

  • Gatto’s great-great-grandfather, Giovanni Gatto, was forced out of Italy for his beliefs and actions.
    • He was a Presbyterian, a journalist known for stirring up trouble, and a Freemason.
    • His wife, Lucrezia, lost her inheritance and title for marrying him.
  • The Gatto family moved to Pittsburgh and found success.
    • Giovanni was hired by Andrew Mellon to work at Mellon Bank.
    • They hosted the famous tenor Enrico Caruso when he visited Pittsburgh.
  • Their prosperity didn’t last.
    • Giovanni died at age 49 due to an extravagant lifestyle.
    • Lucrezia buried him in an unmarked grave because he had a mistress.

Harry Taylor Zimmer: The Contrarian Republican

  • Gatto’s great-grandfather, Harry Taylor Zimmer, was a unique character.
    • He was a printer, a circus owner, and a staunch Republican in a predominantly Democratic town.
    • He ran for mayor multiple times, using his printing press to publish harsh attacks on his opponents.
    • During World War II, he would publicly support Germany, much to the dismay of the townspeople.
  • Gatto’s uncle, Bud Zimmer, was a respected figure in town and protected him from bullies.

Bud Zimmer: From War Hero to Industry Leader

  • Bud Zimmer served as a field officer in World War II, fighting in the Battle of the Bulge.
  • He was featured in newsreels, driving a jeep named “Monongahela.”
  • One of the soldiers under his command was Al Rockwell, who later became a successful industrialist.
  • Al Rockwell gave Bud a management position at his company despite Bud’s lack of college education.

A Chip Off the Old Block

  • Gatto acknowledges that Christina is following in her family’s footsteps by being independent and forging her own path.
  • He advises Christina to think carefully about attending Dartmouth or any elite institution.
    • He believes that the prestige associated with such schools is an illusion.
    • He cautions her against being seduced by the idea of social privilege, arguing that it ultimately leads to a restrictive life.
  • Gatto encourages Christina to question the prevailing belief that attending a top college guarantees a successful life.
    • He points out that no one can definitively explain how these institutions transform individuals.

The Gospel of Elite Education

  • Gatto argues that the education system, particularly elite high schools, indoctrinates students into believing that attending prestigious colleges is essential for success.
  • He criticizes the pressure placed on students during the college admissions process, noting that some are driven to despair and even suicide by the fear of rejection.
  • Gatto shares his own experience of being rejected by Duke University and his relief at being accepted by Cornell, highlighting the prevailing elitism in higher education.

The Malignancy of Elitism

  • Gatto condemns the elitist mindset prevalent in American society, arguing that it undermines the country’s founding principle of equality.
  • He cites George Orwell’s “Animal Farm” to illustrate the hypocrisy of claiming equality while favoring certain groups over others.
  • He urges Christina to reject this “moral cancer” and embrace the true American ideal of equal opportunity for all.

Finding Value Beyond Wealth and Status

  • Gatto challenges the notion that wealth and status are the ultimate measures of success.
  • He argues that true value lies in finding purpose and contributing to society in meaningful ways.
  • He uses the example of a hot dog vendor who earns more than prominent figures to illustrate that financial success can be found outside traditional paths.

The True Measure of Education

  • Gatto emphasizes the importance of education but argues that true learning extends beyond formal schooling.
  • He stresses the need for critical thinking skills to navigate the complexities of modern society.
  • He outlines eight essential aspects of real education:
    1. Self-knowledge: Understanding one’s character, strengths, weaknesses, and motivations.
    2. Observation: Developing sharp observational skills and the ability to analyze situations objectively.
    3. Feedback: Being receptive to feedback from others and using it for self-improvement.
    4. Analysis: Breaking down complex problems into smaller components and identifying solutions.
    5. Mirroring: Developing empathy and the ability to understand different perspectives.
    6. Expression: Communicating effectively both in writing and speaking.
    7. Judgment: Discerning truth from falsehood and making sound decisions.
    8. Adding Value: Contributing positively to every interaction and group effort.

Dartmouth’s Curriculum of Conformity

  • Gatto argues that institutions like Dartmouth prioritize teaching students how to conform to the expectations of those in power.
  • He suggests that students are subtly conditioned to adopt the values, beliefs, and behaviors favored by the ruling class.
  • He warns Christina that this indoctrination can lead to a disconnect from one’s parents and a sense of alienation from those who haven’t undergone the same conditioning.

The Limitations of Contrived Problems

  • Gatto criticizes the emphasis on the case study method at Dartmouth, arguing that it creates a false sense of mastery over complex real-world issues.
  • He contends that this approach fails to account for the unpredictable nature of human behavior and the limitations of applying theoretical solutions to real-world problems.
  • He uses the examples of Iraq, Afghanistan, and Sudan as evidence of the failures of applying simplistic solutions to complex geopolitical situations.

Trapped in a Labyrinth

  • Gatto asserts that the current education system is inadequate for addressing the challenges facing the nation.
  • He believes that both Christina’s generation and the country as a whole are trapped in a system that no longer serves their best interests.

A Nation Beyond Schooling’s Reach

  • Gatto argues that the problems facing the United States extend beyond the scope of what traditional education can solve.
  • He cites social unrest, a decline in faith in American leadership, and the erosion of the middle class as symptoms of a deeper societal malaise.

Social Unrest and the Erosion of Faith

  • Gatto attributes the decline in trust in American institutions to the actions of corporations and the political elite.
  • He contends that these entities have prioritized their own interests over the well-being of the American people, leading to widespread disillusionment.

The Unilateral Reorganization of Work

  • Gatto criticizes the restructuring of the American workforce, arguing that it has led to job insecurity, a decline in wages, and the erosion of the middle class.
  • He blames corporations and their allies in government and academia for implementing policies that benefit the wealthy at the expense of the working class.

The Rise of Contingent Labor

  • Gatto points to the rise of contingent labor, where workers are hired on a temporary or contract basis, as a symptom of the decline in job security.
  • He argues that this practice creates a precarious workforce with limited benefits and no long-term stability.

The Dehumanizing Impact of Lean Production

  • Gatto criticizes the concept of lean production, which aims to maximize efficiency by minimizing waste and reducing labor costs.
  • He argues that this approach dehumanizes workers, treating them as expendable resources rather than valuable assets.

The New Atlantis: College as Utility

  • Gatto argues that higher education has been transformed into a system designed to produce workers who conform to the needs of corporations and government bureaucracies.

The Transformation of Higher Education

  • Gatto contends that the focus of higher education has shifted from fostering critical thinking and intellectual exploration to training students for specific roles in the workforce.

Bacon’s Vision of Control

  • Gatto cites Francis Bacon’s “The New Atlantis” as a blueprint for using universities to maintain social order and control.
  • He argues that Bacon’s vision has been adopted by those in power to create a system of education that reinforces existing hierarchies.

The Dream of Total Social Control

  • Gatto suggests that the transformation of higher education is part of a larger effort to exert greater control over society.
  • He argues that institutions like Dartmouth play a role in shaping the beliefs and values of future leaders, ensuring that they will uphold the existing power structure.

Surveillance and the Suppression of Dissent

  • Gatto contends that modern society is characterized by increasing surveillance and a suppression of dissent.
  • He argues that this trend is driven by a desire to maintain social order and prevent challenges to the status quo.

A Managed America

  • Gatto argues that the United States is becoming increasingly rule-bound and controlled.

  • Gatto suggests that those in power have become more sophisticated in their methods of control, using a combination of economic incentives and social pressure to maintain order.

  • Gatto contends that the education system has created a divide between children and their families by promoting values and beliefs that often conflict with those taught at home.

Chapter 10: Incident at Highland High

Schooling and Education

  • Pedagogy: A term from Roman days designating a special class of slave.
    • Gatto feels his life has been lived under the influence of pedagogy, as a character in someone else’s script.
    • He hopes his book will help readers escape a similar destiny.
  • Gatto’s ambition is to provoke readers into analyzing their own schooling and the price paid for it.
    • He wants readers to question the illusions of education that schooling produces.
    • He wants readers to recognize how schooling’s influence continues long after formal education ends.
  • Schooling has expanded its reach over time.
    • It began as a limited endeavor in colonial America (two hours a day, a few months a year).
    • Today, it strives to become a total institution, resenting even summer vacation.
    • “Lifelong learning” really means “lifelong schooling.”
  • More schooling is often presented as the solution to societal problems.
    • Gatto questions this, suggesting that less school may be the cause of some of these problems.
  • Schooling undermines other avenues of development:
    • Family: Seen as a retrograde institution to be replaced.
    • Church: Discouraged as it presents the idea of a higher power than experts.
    • Tradition, ethnic loyalty, loyalty to place: All dismissed.
  • Schooling damages conceptions of individual sovereignty and liberty.
    • This damage worsens with each generation as the ability to parent effectively is diminished.
  • Key Differences Between Schooling and Education:
    • Organization:
      • Schooling: Command and control from without.
      • Education: Self-organized from within.
    • Sources of Learning:
      • Schooling: Disconnects clientele from other primary sources.
      • Education: Provides bountiful, random, and diverse connections, fostering resourcefulness and self-sufficiency.
    • Feedback Loops:
      • Schooling: Emphasizes rules made by others.
      • Education: Awakens students to the importance of natural feedback loops for self-correction and independence.
    • Focus:
      • Schooling: Committed to subject knowledge, creating clerks and specialists.
      • Education: Regards things in context, recognizing the power of cross-fertilization and diverse perspectives.
    • Mindset:
      • Schooling: Emphasizes memory and acceptance of orthodox theory.
      • Education: Cultivates a state of dialectical argument, questioning assumptions and seeking new understandings.
  • The International Happiness Survey identifies three conditions for a happy life:
    1. Good relationships
    2. Good health
    3. Satisfying work
  • Schooling undermines all three:
    • Health: School environments often make good health difficult.
    • Relationships: Limited time and social segregation hinder relationship development.
    • Work: Schoolwork rarely addresses the compelling questions of youth.

Questioning the Critique of Schooling

  • Gatto acknowledges that his critique may seem harsh.
    • He questions whether his metaphors are exaggerated or if schools are truly reformable.
    • He acknowledges that the historical figures who shaped schooling may not have intended harm.
  • Gatto concludes that a deliberate intent exists within the school institution.
    • He believes this intent operates hidden from public view.
    • He argues that “school reform” is meaningless without confronting the true ends of the system.
  • Gatto challenges readers to investigate for themselves, arguing that they must reach their own conclusions.

Three Hair-Raising School Stories

  • Gatto presents three stories to illustrate the “dark force” he perceives within schooling.
  • He challenges readers to interpret these stories and consider who benefits from the actions described.
  • He emphasizes that these stories are not isolated incidents, suggesting a systemic problem.

Incident at Nuremberg

  • Date: January 29, 2008
  • Location: Nuremberg, Germany
  • Summary: 16-year-old Melissa Besekrus was forcibly removed from her home by police and subjected to psychiatric evaluation for homeschooling.
  • Details:
    • Homeschooling was banned in Germany in 1937 by the Nazi government.
    • Gatto wrote to the German ambassador questioning the continued enforcement of this ban.
    • Melissa was interrogated for 240 minutes about her reasons for homeschooling.
    • The interrogation concluded she suffered from “school phobia,” a diagnosis used to justify her removal from her family.
    • Gatto criticizes the German court order, the use of force, and the flimsy justification for intervention.
    • He draws parallels between this incident and Germany’s history of authoritarianism and suppression of individual liberty.
    • Gatto received no response from the German ambassador.

Incident at Highland High

  • Date: March 25, 2004
  • Location: Highland High School, Rockland County, New York
  • Summary: Gatto was invited to speak at Highland High but his talk was interrupted by police, called by the superintendent, who deemed it inflammatory.
  • Details:
    • Gatto was invited by school board member John Jankiewicz, who was concerned about the effects of traditional schooling.
    • Gatto’s talk aimed to debunk myths about college admissions and encourage students to think critically about their education.
    • He presented data challenging the effectiveness of standardized tests and highlighting the achievements of individuals who didn’t follow traditional paths.
    • During his talk, police entered the auditorium and ordered everyone to leave.
    • The superintendent, McCarthy, had called the police because he found the talk inflammatory.
    • Gatto was escorted from the building.
    • An evening talk for parents was also canceled.
    • The local newspaper reported the incident but omitted the police involvement.
    • The superintendent claimed Gatto showed a “violent film,” which was untrue.
    • Gatto sent letters to school officials and the student newspaper requesting their perspectives, but received no response.

Incident at Walden

  • Date: April 1991
  • Location: Walden, Vermont
  • Summary: The state of Vermont forced the closure of four one-room schoolhouses despite local opposition.
  • Details:
    • Walden had four well-regarded one-room schools with above-average academic performance.
    • The state threatened to withhold funding and seize the schools through eminent domain if they were not made handicap accessible, claiming exorbitant costs.
    • Gatto visited and was impressed by the schools and the students.
    • An architect confirmed that the state’s cost estimates were highly inflated.
    • The architect refused to testify, fearing retribution from the state.
    • Gatto spoke at a town meeting, but fear of the state’s threats led to the bond issue passing.
    • The one-room schools were replaced with a large, unattractive regional school.

Conclusion: Sabotaging the System

  • Gatto urges readers to recognize the “dark world” of schooling and help dismantle the system.
  • He argues that schooling, like certain large companies, is considered too important to fail, preventing real educational alternatives from flourishing.
